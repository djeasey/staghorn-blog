Kidney Stones 101: Struvites & Staghorns

Written for non-medical folk: Basic kidney function and disease process causing kidney stones. Discussion of role of dehydration, salts, and the urinary tract.

What Even Are Kidney Stones?

What Should The Kidneys Do?

The human body is, in a manner of speaking, a walking sack of blood. When we talk about how much blood we usually cite the number 5L. Not perfect, but near enough for us. Every day the heart pumps this around your body at an amazing rate: 7200L pumping through your heart daily. 

Of that 7200L, 75% is sent by the heart to the tissues of the body. This blood brings the brain, muscles, and organs their supply of oxygen, sugar, and all the chemicals they needs. Meanwhile the blood carries away all of the garbage from the cells that they don’t want, chiefly: carbon dioxide, urea, creatinine, various salts, and all the excess water and other chemicals they've absorbed or created. Now before we go any further, know that when I say “salts” I’m not necessarily talking about the those white crystals you might add to your dinner, rather the category of chemical compounds of which table salt (iodinised NaCl) is a member.

The remaining 25% (1800L/day) goes to the kidneys. Their job is to let all the fluid bits of the blood into their tubules and keep all the big bulky cells and proteins in the blood. From these tubules, the important stuff that your body wants to hold onto is reabsorbed back into the blood. The remaining fluid, the unwanted salts, chemicals, and toxins are passed to the bladder and eventually urinated out.

However, the handy thing is that the kidneys can absorb things differently depending on what the body needs. If you’re dehydrated, they will reabsorb more water, making more concentrated urine. Thus, the bladder fills with a more concentrated, darker urine, that’s packed with more of those salts, toxins/waste chemicals, and lose less of that precious water.

To be honest, I think that’s pretty cool.

What Shouldn’t They Do?

However to keep doctors in business, sometimes things in the body have to go wrong. Thankfully for the doctors, the human body is magnificent... but beautifully imperfect.

When the kidneys are reabsorbing the water they want (from inside the kidney’s tubules back into the blood), they concentrate all those unwanted chemicals. Sometimes the highly concentrated toxins/salts can play havoc on the kidneys. Such chemicals can include some toxins, some medications, and some salts absorbed from food: 

  • Calcium oxalate, the most common cause of kidney stones, formed from the breakdown of a chemical in some foods.
  • Struvite, commonly forming stones in women with frequent UTIs (the most common cause of large multi-branched stones called STAGHORN calculi (calculi being Latin for stones).
  • Uric acid, the chemical that causes the crystals in gout.
  • Cysteine, typically in people with the genetic disorder cystinuria.

When one of these dissolved salts is highly concentrated it can crystallise INSIDE the tubules of the kidney. What this means, is that like a shiny urinary snowball, a small number of these salt molecules clump together to form a solid stone. This stone gets bigger and bigger as more salt molecules join the party. Next thing you know, there’s a massive stone sitting in your kidney.

A Stone!! Now What?

In short. It’s not great. Pain. And often infections.

Just like normal urine the body tries to pass the stones from the kidneys, down connecting pipes called the ureters, into the bladder. From the bladder, when it’s time to urinate, the body aims to pass the urine (and any stones) through the urethra to the outside world (and hopefully flushed down the plumbing). However typically, somewhere in this process the stone can get stuck. This hurts. A lot, many women comparing it (and describing it as worse that) childbirth.

Whilst smaller stones can often pass on their own (with a weirdly strong degree of evidence suggesting this are aided greatly by a patient going on a roller coaster), larger stones may need medical, interventional, or even surgical help.

As always, if you think you might have a kidney stone, are in any pain, or are concerned about your health in the short or long term, go see a doctor. If you're in crippling agony, skip down to your local Emergency Department post haste. If you're not currently in extreme pain but have some concerns, go see your GP.

If you don't have a regulary GP, which I absolutely recommend you do if you can, I suggest you find an affordable/bulk-billing clinic to regularly attend just so you are on their local files. This IMMENSELY helps the doctors do their jobs better.
